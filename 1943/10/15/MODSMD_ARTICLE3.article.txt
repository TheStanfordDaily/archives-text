# Fourth President A Stanford Man
## 
### 
Stanford's fourth president, Dr.
Donald B. Tresidder, is a Stanford
man through and through. His Stan
ford relationships are as undergrad
uate and medical student and assist
ant, the years 1915-1827, except for
the last war, and thereafter constant
services of many kinds.
He is a charter member of Stan
ford Associates, and has served as
vice-president of the organization for
six years. He has been a member of
the Stanford Fund Committee since
its establishment. He was co-chair
man of the 50th Anniversary Com
mittee in 1941, and is chairman of
the Committee on Exceptional Stu
dents.
Board of Trustees
In December 1939, he was elected a
member of the Board of Trustees of
the University, and was chosen pres
ident of the board in May, 1942.
''My chief interest in public serv
ice," he wrote years ago to a friend,
"'is Stanford."
Dr. Tresidder was born in Tipton,
Indiana, April 7, 1894, the son of Dr.
and Mrs. James Treloar Tresidder.
His father, mother, and uncle were
practicing physicians. The son, as a
small boy, often went along; his ear
liest ambition was to be a physician
and in this ambition he persisted.
Comes to California
After a year in a pre-medical
course at the University of Chicago,
he came to California with his sister.
While his train was held up by a
wreck, he became acquainted with a
representative of David A. Curry,
an educator who had established a
summer camp in Yosemite.
This led to a summer's employment
at Camp Curry where a Stanford
professor invited him to visit the
University on his way home.
He was offered board and room in
the home of Professor R. D. Green of
the Department of Mathematics in
exchange for services as cook, he took
the job and hired an Oriental to do
the cooking until he learned how. A
long chain of pre-medical students
inherited the cook's job at the Green
home, from this beginning.
World War Flyer
In 1917, he enlisted in a base hos
pital unit, but after some months of
service at Camp Fremont, Menlo
Park, transferred to the air service
of the Signal Corps as a flying cadet.
In 1918, he received a commission as
a second lieutenant and after further
training in Texas and Illinois, was
transferred to a bombing squadron at
Ellington Field, Houston, Texas,
where the Armistice found him.
Back at Stanford, he became an
assistant in the Physiology Depart
ment and later in the Department of
Anatomy. He was elected to Sigma
Xi. an honorary research society. In
1919 he received the A.B. degree,
and moved to the Stanford Medical
School and received the M.D. degree
m 1927.
Marries Mary Curry
A romance that started in Yosem
ite culminated June 16, 1920 in the
marriage of Donald Tresidder and
Mary Curry, daughter of Mr. and
Mrs. David A. Curry, the founders
of Camp Curry.
Mary Curry had entered Stanford
with the class of 1916 but had com
pleted the undergraduate course in
three years, majoring in English. She
was a member of Kappa Alpha Theta
sorority, Phi Beta Kappa, Theta Sig
ma Phi, and Cap and Gown honorary
societies.
She received the M.A. degree from
Stanford in 1916 and had completed
all requirements for the Ph.D. degree
in English at Yale University except
the writing of her final thesis, which
was interrupted by the engagement
and marriage.
In Yosemite, Dr. Tresidder's
achievements include the establish
ment of the High Sierra camps and
the pioneering of winter sports. Both
Dr. and Mrs. Tresidder are authori
ties on Yosemite geology, flora, and
fauna, and Mrs. Tresidder has writ
ten a book about Yosemite trees.
Dr. Tresidder lists his favorite
recreations as skiing, horseback rid
ing, flying, and camping. Both he
and Mrs. Tresidder use their air
planes a good deal in normal times
for business trips and for recreation.
Their home is in Yosemite, and their
campus home is the Hoover home.

course at the University of Chicago,
he came to California with his sister.
While his train was held up by a
wreck, he became acquainted with a
representative of David A. Curry,
an educator who had established a
summer camp in Yosemite.
This led to a summer's employment
at Camp Curry where a Stanford
professor invited him to visit the
University on his way home.
He was offered board and room in
the home of Professor R. D. Green of
the Department of Mathematics in
exchange for services as cook, he took
the job and hired an Oriental to do
the cooking until he learned how. A
long chain of pre-medical students
inherited the cook's job at the Green
home, from this beginning.
World War Flyer
In 1917, he enlisted in a base hos
pital unit, but after some months of
service at Camp Fremont, Menlo
Park, transferred to the air service
of the Signal Corps as a flying cadet.
In 1918, he received a commission as
a second lieutenant and after further
training in Texas and Illinois, was
transferred to a bombing squadron at
Ellington Field, Houston, Texas,
where the Armistice found him.
Back at Stanford, he became an
assistant in the Physiology Depart
ment and later in the Department of
Anatomy. He was elected to Sigma
Xi. an honorary research society. In
1919 he received the A.B. degree,
and moved to the Stanford Medical
School and received the M.D. degree
m 1927.
Marries Mary Curry
A romance that started in Yosem
ite culminated June 16, 1920 in the
marriage of Donald Tresidder and
Mary Curry, daughter of Mr. and
Mrs. David A. Curry, the founders
of Camp Curry.
Mary Curry had entered Stanford
with the class of 1916 but had com
pleted the undergraduate course in
three years, majoring in English. She
was a member of Kappa Alpha Theta
sorority, Phi Beta Kappa, Theta Sig
ma Phi, and Cap and Gown honorary
societies.
She received the M.A. degree from
Stanford in 1916 and had completed
all requirements for the Ph.D. degree
in English at Yale University except
the writing of her final thesis, which
was interrupted by the engagement
and marriage.
In Yosemite, Dr. Tresidder's
achievements include the establish
ment of the High Sierra camps and
the pioneering of winter sports. Both
Dr. and Mrs. Tresidder are authori
ties on Yosemite geology, flora, and
fauna, and Mrs. Tresidder has writ
ten a book about Yosemite trees.
Dr. Tresidder lists his favorite
recreations as skiing, horseback rid
ing, flying, and camping. Both he
and Mrs. Tresidder use their air
planes a good deal in normal times
for business trips and for recreation.
Their home is in Yosemite, and their
campus home is the Hoover home.
Stanford's fourth president, Dr.
Donald B. Tresidder, is a Stanford
man through and through. His Stan
ford relationships are as undergrad
uate and medical student and assist
ant, the years 1915-1827, except for
the last war, and thereafter constant
services of many kinds.
He is a charter member of Stan
ford Associates, and has served as
vice-president of the organization for
six years. He has been a member of
the Stanford Fund Committee since
its establishment. He was co-chair
man of the 50th Anniversary Com
mittee in 1941, and is chairman of
the Committee on Exceptional Stu
dents.
Board of Trustees
In December 1939, he was elected a
member of the Board of Trustees of
the University, and was chosen pres
ident of the board in May, 1942.
''My chief interest in public serv
ice," he wrote years ago to a friend,
"'is Stanford."

Donald B. Tresidder, is a Stanford
man through and through. His Stan
ford relationships are as undergrad
uate and medical student and assist
ant, the years 1915-1827, except for
the last war, and thereafter constant
services of many kinds.
He is a charter member of Stan
ford Associates, and has served as
vice-president of the organization for
six years. He has been a member of
the Stanford Fund Committee since
its establishment. He was co-chair
man of the 50th Anniversary Com
mittee in 1941, and is chairman of
the Committee on Exceptional Stu
dents.
Board of Trustees
In December 1939, he was elected a
member of the Board of Trustees of
the University, and was chosen pres
ident of the board in May, 1942.
''My chief interest in public serv
ice," he wrote years ago to a friend,
"'is Stanford."
Dr. Tresidder was born in Tipton,
Indiana, April 7, 1894, the son of Dr.
and Mrs. James Treloar Tresidder.
His father, mother, and uncle were
practicing physicians. The son, as a
small boy, often went along; his ear
liest ambition was to be a physician
and in this ambition he persisted.
Comes to California
After a year in a pre-medical
course at the University of Chicago,
he came to California with his sister.
While his train was held up by a
wreck, he became acquainted with a
representative of David A. Curry,
an educator who had established a
summer camp in Yosemite.
This led to a summer's employment
at Camp Curry where a Stanford
professor invited him to visit the
University on his way home.
He was offered board and room in
the home of Professor R. D. Green of
the Department of Mathematics in
exchange for services as cook, he took
the job and hired an Oriental to do
the cooking until he learned how. A
long chain of pre-medical students
inherited the cook's job at the Green
home, from this beginning.
World War Flyer
In 1917, he enlisted in a base hos
pital unit, but after some months of
service at Camp Fremont, Menlo
Park, transferred to the air service
of the Signal Corps as a flying cadet.
In 1918, he received a commission as
a second lieutenant and after further
training in Texas and Illinois, was
transferred to a bombing squadron at
Ellington Field, Houston, Texas,
where the Armistice found him.
Back at Stanford, he became an
assistant in the Physiology Depart
ment and later in the Department of
Anatomy. He was elected to Sigma
Xi. an honorary research society. In
1919 he received the A.B. degree,
and moved to the Stanford Medical
School and received the M.D. degree
m 1927.
Marries Mary Curry
A romance that started in Yosem
ite culminated June 16, 1920 in the
marriage of Donald Tresidder and
Mary Curry, daughter of Mr. and
Mrs. David A. Curry, the founders
of Camp Curry.
Mary Curry had entered Stanford
with the class of 1916 but had com
pleted the undergraduate course in
three years, majoring in English. She
was a member of Kappa Alpha Theta
sorority, Phi Beta Kappa, Theta Sig
ma Phi, and Cap and Gown honorary
societies.
She received the M.A. degree from
Stanford in 1916 and had completed
all requirements for the Ph.D. degree
in English at Yale University except
the writing of her final thesis, which
was interrupted by the engagement
and marriage.
In Yosemite, Dr. Tresidder's
achievements include the establish
ment of the High Sierra camps and
the pioneering of winter sports. Both
Dr. and Mrs. Tresidder are authori
ties on Yosemite geology, flora, and
fauna, and Mrs. Tresidder has writ
ten a book about Yosemite trees.
Dr. Tresidder lists his favorite
recreations as skiing, horseback rid
ing, flying, and camping. Both he
and Mrs. Tresidder use their air
planes a good deal in normal times
for business trips and for recreation.
Their home is in Yosemite, and their
campus home is the Hoover home.
