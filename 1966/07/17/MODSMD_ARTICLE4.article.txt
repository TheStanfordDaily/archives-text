### Teri Seibert 
Stanford's Committee for
Peace in Vietnam sponsored
a two day teach-in July 7 and 8
in Tresidder's large lounge.
Eight speakers, followed by
a discussion and question
period, highlighted the three
and one half hour sessions.
The SCPV is a group which
was organized last year at Stan
ford to protest the United States
position in Vietnam.
Christian Bay
Christian Bay, a lecturer in
political science, began Thurs
day's session giving a general
background and history of Viet
nam. After tracing the history
of Vietnam from 1940 to the
present, Bay discussed the U.
S. position in the Vietnam war.
Bay explained that United
States withdrawal from Vietnam
is based on what the U. S.
hopes to achieve there. He list
ed the terms of the National
Liberation Front as the key
to sucess in Vietnam. The terms
include electing a new national
assembly, granting amnesty to
political prisoners, revitalizing
agriculture, organizing social
welfare, guaranteeing property
rights, establishing diplomatic
relations with all countries re

gardless of their political
status, plus other measures.
Withdrawal
Bay stated that U. S. refusal
to accept the principles of the
National Liberation Front is
keeping this country from with
drawing from Vietnam.
His speech was followed by
former SCPC Chairman Cyril
Sia's discussion of the legal
history of Vietnam.
Also on Thursday, Bruce

English, talked on peace feelers
