# Campus Roundup
## 
### 
The Student-Faculty board and Symposium Committee will pre
sent a SPRING SYMPOSIUM on April 23 at 7:15 in Dinkelspiel
Auditorium. There will be discussion seminars in the homes of
faculty and staff members on topics ranging from "Undergraduate
education, relevance for what?" to "Myth and symbolism." Sign
ups for the seminars are today, from 9:00 to 1:00 in front of the
Post Office, or by phoning Fred Fink or Buck Parker at 323-6485 in
the evening.
★ ★ ★
Tuesday night at 7:30 in Tresidder the OVERSEAS CAMPUS
BOARD is holding its second quarterly open meeting. The purpose
of such meetings is to allow all members of the Stanford commu
nity to express their concerns about the Overseas Campuses pro
gram and to ask questions about how the program is operated.
An important function of the meeting will be to organize the
"Overseas Campuses Week" to be held early next fall. Through the
Overseas Campuses Week the board hopes to be able to give fresh
men a more valid overview of the campuses than they now seem
to have before applying.
★ ★ ★
The Experiment will sponsor a performance of MICHAEL Mc-
CLURE'S CONTROVERSIAL PI.AY, THE BEARD, Wednesday
evening at 8:30 p.m. in Dinkelspiel Auditorium. The production
stars the original two-person cast of Richard Bright as Billy the
Kid and Billy Dixon as Jean Harlow, admission is $2.50. Tickets are
available at Tresidder Box Office.
★ ★ ★
Today there will be two benefit presentations for the COMMIT
TEE FOR THE RESCUE OF ITALIAN ART (CRIA) in the Miedel
Room of the Stanford Art Museum at 10:00 a.m. and 8:00 p.m. Pro
fessor Matthew Kahn of the Department of Art and Architecture,
who was in residence at Stanford's campus in Florence at the time
of the floods this autumn, will give a lecture entitled "An Eyewit
ness Account of the Flood and its Effects," illustrated by color
slides. Coffee will be served after the lecture. Contributions for
CRIA will be solicited.
★ ★ ★
On Sunday, May 28, the PHILADELPHIA SYMPHONY OR
CHESTRA will perform in Frost Amphitheater at 4:30 p.m. Ticket
sales for the Stanford community begin today at Tresidder Box
Office, and become open to the general public on April 23.
★ ★ ★
The Music Department will present two lectures this week. Sir
Jack Westrup, distinguished BRITISH MUSICOLOGIST, will lec
ture on "The Paradox of 18th Century Music" at 4:15 today at
Dinkelspiel Auditorium. This Thursday Alan Rich, music critic for
the New York Times, will present a talk entitled "The Music Critic
—Saviour or Scrouge?" at 4:15 p.m. in Cubberley Auditorium.
★ ★ ★
A THREE-DAY MEETING ON "THE PHYSICAL PROPERTIES
OF IRON PROTEINS," opening today, features an international
list of 16 scientists headed by a British Nobel Prize winner. Profes
sor Max F. Perutz, 1962 Nobel laureate in chemistry, will give two
lectures on "The Structure of Crystalline Proteins" today and
Wednesday. Other speakers include scientists from England, Japan,
and various U.S. research centers. Their talks will cover X-ray dif
fraction, magnetic susceptibility, electron spin resonance, optical
and far infrared absorption, and related subjects.
★ ★ ★
Dr. Harry B. Gray, Professor of Chemistry at the California In
stitute of Technology, has been named the eighth recipient of the
annual EDWARD C. FRANKLIN MEMORIAL AWARD, spon
sored by the lota chapter of Phi Lambda Upsilon, honorary chemi
cal society and the Chemistry Department of Stanford University.
Today at 4:00 p.m. in Rm 20 of the Chemistry building, Dr. Gray
will be presented the bronze medallion in recognition of his out
standing contributions to chemistry.
Dr. Gray's research interests include ligand field theory, in
organic reaction mechanisms, and spectral and magnetic properties
of transition metal complexes using molecular-orbital theory.

★ ★ ★
A THREE-DAY MEETING ON "THE PHYSICAL PROPERTIES
OF IRON PROTEINS," opening today, features an international
list of 16 scientists headed by a British Nobel Prize winner. Profes
sor Max F. Perutz, 1962 Nobel laureate in chemistry, will give two
lectures on "The Structure of Crystalline Proteins" today and
Wednesday. Other speakers include scientists from England, Japan,
and various U.S. research centers. Their talks will cover X-ray dif
fraction, magnetic susceptibility, electron spin resonance, optical
and far infrared absorption, and related subjects.
★ ★ ★
Dr. Harry B. Gray, Professor of Chemistry at the California In
stitute of Technology, has been named the eighth recipient of the
annual EDWARD C. FRANKLIN MEMORIAL AWARD, spon
sored by the lota chapter of Phi Lambda Upsilon, honorary chemi
cal society and the Chemistry Department of Stanford University.
Today at 4:00 p.m. in Rm 20 of the Chemistry building, Dr. Gray
will be presented the bronze medallion in recognition of his out
standing contributions to chemistry.
Dr. Gray's research interests include ligand field theory, in
organic reaction mechanisms, and spectral and magnetic properties
of transition metal complexes using molecular-orbital theory.
The Student-Faculty board and Symposium Committee will pre
sent a SPRING SYMPOSIUM on April 23 at 7:15 in Dinkelspiel
Auditorium. There will be discussion seminars in the homes of
faculty and staff members on topics ranging from "Undergraduate
education, relevance for what?" to "Myth and symbolism." Sign
ups for the seminars are today, from 9:00 to 1:00 in front of the
Post Office, or by phoning Fred Fink or Buck Parker at 323-6485 in
the evening.
