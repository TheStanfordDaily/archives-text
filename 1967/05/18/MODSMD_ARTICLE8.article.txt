# Campus Roundup
##

MELIVIN GURTOV, a member of the staff of the RAND Cor
poration, and author of the recently published book "The First
Vietnam Crisis" will speak on "China and Southeast Asia: Policies
Toward Vietnam, Thailand, and Cambodia." The talk, at 4:15 in
Tresidder room 270, is sponsored by Stanford's East Asian Studies
Committee.
★ ★ ★
An analysis of contemporary Italian manners and morals in the
film "I NUOVI ANGELI" directed by Ugo Gregoretti, will be shown
at 8:15 p.m. in Tresidder room 274.
Focusing on the younger set, the film shows a younger genera
tion of Italians uprooted from the past and uncertain about the
future. Coffee and discussion with Cesare Masserenti will follow
the presentation.
★ ★ ★
FOLK BLUES WORKSHOP begins at the ASSUCH tonight from
9 p.m. to 12:30 p.m. Featuring an open microphone, the workshop
will be a regular Thursday night feature for the Coffee House.
★ ★ ★
WORKSHOP, the undergraduate literary magazine, will be on
sale today at Engineering Corner, Tresidder Union, and the Post
Office. The price is ten cents.
This second issue of the quarter includes the work of Bruce
Block, Gregory White, James Williams, Steve Siporin, Tim Qu'ealy,
and Paul Wrightman. There are six poems and one short story.
★ ★ ★
A panel discussion of Stanford's Involvement in the Military In
dustrial Complex will be held in the Clubhouse at noon. Ira Arlook,
David Ransom, and William Rambo, Associate Dean of the School
of Engineering, will participate in the discussion. The panel is
being sponsored by the YWCA as part of its Discussion series.
★ ★ ★
An exhibition of the work of WILLIAM MORRIS, English artist,
poet, printer, and furniture manufacturer of the late Victorian era,
opens in the Bender Room of the Stanford Main Library at 3:30
p.m. today.
Speakers will be S. Dale Harris, assistant professor of English,
and Ward Ritchie of the Ward Ritchie Press, Los Angeles.
"The products of William Morris' busy life — romances in prose
and verse, drawings, designs for furniture, carpets, tapestries, wall
paper, chintzes, tiles, stained glass, the handsome volumes that
issued from the Kelmscott Press — were examples of determined
protest," said University archivist Ralph Wihansen.
★ ★ ★

February Revolution" at 3:15 p.m. today in Hoover Tower Seminar
room 116.
Dr. Katkov, born in Moscow in 1903, and a Russian resident un
til 1921, is a visiting scholar at the Hoover Institution.
MELIVIN GURTOV, a member of the staff of the RAND Cor
poration, and author of the recently published book "The First
Vietnam Crisis" will speak on "China and Southeast Asia: Policies
Toward Vietnam, Thailand, and Cambodia." The talk, at 4:15 in
Tresidder room 270, is sponsored by Stanford's East Asian Studies
Committee.
★ ★ ★
An analysis of contemporary Italian manners and morals in the
film "I NUOVI ANGELI" directed by Ugo Gregoretti, will be shown
at 8:15 p.m. in Tresidder room 274.
Focusing on the younger set, the film shows a younger genera
tion of Italians uprooted from the past and uncertain about the
future. Coffee and discussion with Cesare Masserenti will follow
the presentation.

9 p.m. to 12:30 p.m. Featuring an open microphone, the workshop
will be a regular Thursday night feature for the Coffee House.

A panel discussion of Stanford's Involvement in the Military In
dustrial Complex will be held in the Clubhouse at noon. Ira Arlook,
David Ransom, and William Rambo, Associate Dean of the School
of Engineering, will participate in the discussion. The panel is
being sponsored by the YWCA as part of its Discussion series.
★ ★ ★
An exhibition of the work of WILLIAM MORRIS, English artist,
poet, printer, and furniture manufacturer of the late Victorian era,
opens in the Bender Room of the Stanford Main Library at 3:30
p.m. today.
Speakers will be S. Dale Harris, assistant professor of English,
and Ward Ritchie of the Ward Ritchie Press, Los Angeles.
"The products of William Morris' busy life — romances in prose
and verse, drawings, designs for furniture, carpets, tapestries, wall
paper, chintzes, tiles, stained glass, the handsome volumes that
issued from the Kelmscott Press — were examples of determined
protest," said University archivist Ralph Wihansen.
