# Teach-ins explore experiences with racism
## 
### June Fang Staff writer 
His black friends are fol
lowed in the Bookstore and
questioned by police at Lake
Lagunita while white people
are left alone, an administra
tor said.
A black student described
rumors of "reverse racism"
that circulated when his com
pany rapidly promoted him.
And other speakers at yes
terday's teach-in addressed
the institutionalized racism
that led a Southern California
jury to acquit the white police
officers accused of beating
Rodney King.
Several students said the
teach-ins provided an opportu
nity to discuss issues of racism
and inequality on a personal
level.
"This is such a great way to
learn — people talking to other
people about what is important
to them, what offends them,
what they believe in," said
senior Kristan Klingelhofer.
Students said they were
deeply affected by the tales of
personal experiences with
racism, which ranged from
problems in the workplace to
encounters with police.
One black business school
student, who wished to remain
anonymous, said his promo
tion spurred talk of "reverse
racism."
Though other promotions
had not been as heavily scruti
nized, he said, the company
circulated an article on how
much money his work had
saved them in order to justify
his elevation.
In the "Police Brutality,
Community Response" teach
in, Karl Knapper, department
administrator for the Martin
Luther King, Jr. Papers Pro
ject, shared some of his
encounters with racism in law
enforcement.

racism on campus, from

Besides being questioned by

from Palo Alto, Knapper said he
was also frisked by officers at the
Caltrain station because they said

An active member of the Bay
Area homosexual community,
Knapper also spoke about the dif
ferent treatment gays and les
bians receive from law enforce
ment officials.
"As long as one group is
oppressed, we are all oppressed,"
he said.
In the "What Caused the Vio
lence? Institutional Racism or
Mob Mentality?" session, Keith
Archuleta, director of the Black
Community Services Center,
addressed reasons for racist
behavior.
"Institutional racism is the
kind of thing that allows people
who do not wear hoods to perpet
uate racist policies," he said.
"It is very hard in the U.S. to
convict a police officer. A jury is
more likely to see the black per
son who comes into the court
room as a criminal.

"Class, economic and race divi
sions reinforce and interact with
one another. As long as there are
these inequalities, you'll have
racism," Archuleta said.
Some students took time off
from classes to attend 11 differ
ent teach-ins that went on for
most of the day yesterday.
"I didn't skip class, but I would
have," said senior Sarah Long.
Several who attended said they
believe the teach-in was an impor
tant opportunity.
"If people aren't willing to take
time out of their daily schedules
to deal with the issues that con
front us now, they will be put off
until it is too late," freshman said
Blythe Henwood.
Hen wood attended the "Per
sonal Perspectives on Racism in
our Daily Lives" teach-in. "Per
sonal Perspectives" used an
installment from the news pro
gram Prime Time Live to spark a
discussion among the students.
The program, titled "True Col
ors," depicted the different ways
whites and blacks are treated in
everyday activities such as house
searching, employment seeking
and car shopping.
The participants were then
divided into small groups to share
their reactions to the show. Hen
wood said she was affected by the
video and the discussion.
"I wanted to show that (pro
gram) to more people," she added.

Some students took time off
from classes to attend 11 differ
ent teach-ins that went on for

"I didn't skip class, but I would

Several who attended said they
believe the teach-in was an impor
tant opportunity.
"If people aren't willing to take
time out of their daily schedules
to deal with the issues that con
front us now, they will be put off
until it is too late," freshman said
Blythe Henwood.
Hen wood attended the "Per
sonal Perspectives on Racism in
our Daily Lives" teach-in. "Per
sonal Perspectives" used an
installment from the news pro
gram Prime Time Live to spark a
discussion among the students.
The program, titled "True Col
ors," depicted the different ways
whites and blacks are treated in
everyday activities such as house
searching, employment seeking
and car shopping.
The participants were then
divided into small groups to share
their reactions to the show. Hen
wood said she was affected by the
video and the discussion.
"I wanted to show that (pro
gram) to more people," she added.
