April 6, 1999
Dear President Casper,
We, the undersigned campus homeowners, believe that the Faculty Housing Development proposal submitted to you in January
by Geoffrey Cox is not in the University's best interest. Before finalizing plans that would result in the permanent elimination
of much-valued play areas and natural open spaces in our community — for the sake of 36 units that few young faculty will be
able to afford — we urge you to consider alternatives that would represent a more comprehensive planning approach.
Specifically, we ask you to:
(1) Suspend all work on the proposed "infill" developments, pending investigation of the costs and benefits oF the full range
of alternative options including:
a) other sites for housing construction, their capacity, advantages and disadvantages;
b) ways that housing assistance and loan programs for faculty could be improved;
c) policies to reserve housing on Stanford lands for eligible Stanford personnel;
d) policies to give retired faculty and staff priority in Stanford West senior housing;
(2) Report the results of these investigations to all eligible faculty and staff;
(3) After full disclosure and discussion, survey faculty and staff concerning their views of the alternatives;
(4) Strengthen the role of campus residents in the governance of their community.
Over 500 Stanford residents support this request. Their names are listed on the following pages, along with a campus map
showing the broad geographic distribution of households represented.
We want to stress again that we are not against building necessary housing. We are eager to see Stanford's housing needs
addressed, and many campus homeowners are ready to roll up their sleeves and help figure out the best approaches. Many
have already offered constructive suggestions. After all, this is our campus, our institution, and we have shown and will contin
ue to show that we want it to flourish in every way.
But we are for responsible and comprehensive planning that respects the interests of all the constituencies in our community.
Responsible planning, in our view, requires taking into account residents' views concerning proposed developments in their
neighborhoods — in particular the desire to retain sufficient open space and parkland in our community to ensure that these
neighborhoods not only meet the needs of present Stanford families and students but remain an important asset in recruiting
new faculty. We urge you not to preclude the development of a comprehensive and well-integrated campus housing plan by
proceeding in isolation with the "infill" proposal.
We all have much to gain from working together to find housing solutions that serve the interests of the entire University com
munity.
Respectfully, (signatories listed on this page)
cc: Stanford University Board of Trustees

April 6, 1999
Dear President Casper,
We, the undersigned campus homeowners, believe that the Faculty Housing Development proposal submitted to you in January
by Geoffrey Cox is not in the University's best interest. Before finalizing plans that would result in the permanent elimination
of much-valued play areas and natural open spaces in our community — for the sake of 36 units that few young faculty will be
able to afford — we urge you to consider alternatives that would represent a more comprehensive planning approach.
Specifically, we ask you to:
(1) Suspend all work on the proposed "infill" developments, pending investigation of the costs and benefits oF the full range
of alternative options including:
a) other sites for housing construction, their capacity, advantages and disadvantages;
b) ways that housing assistance and loan programs for faculty could be improved;
c) policies to reserve housing on Stanford lands for eligible Stanford personnel;
d) policies to give retired faculty and staff priority in Stanford West senior housing;
(2) Report the results of these investigations to all eligible faculty and staff;
(3) After full disclosure and discussion, survey faculty and staff concerning their views of the alternatives;
(4) Strengthen the role of campus residents in the governance of their community.
Over 500 Stanford residents support this request. Their names are listed on the following pages, along with a campus map
showing the broad geographic distribution of households represented.
We want to stress again that we are not against building necessary housing. We are eager to see Stanford's housing needs
addressed, and many campus homeowners are ready to roll up their sleeves and help figure out the best approaches. Many
have already offered constructive suggestions. After all, this is our campus, our institution, and we have shown and will contin
ue to show that we want it to flourish in every way.
But we are for responsible and comprehensive planning that respects the interests of all the constituencies in our community.
Responsible planning, in our view, requires taking into account residents' views concerning proposed developments in their
neighborhoods — in particular the desire to retain sufficient open space and parkland in our community to ensure that these
neighborhoods not only meet the needs of present Stanford families and students but remain an important asset in recruiting
new faculty. We urge you not to preclude the development of a comprehensive and well-integrated campus housing plan by
proceeding in isolation with the "infill" proposal.
We all have much to gain from working together to find housing solutions that serve the interests of the entire University com
munity.
Respectfully, (signatories listed on this page)

April 6, 1999
Dear President Casper,
We, the undersigned campus homeowners, believe that the Faculty Housing Development proposal submitted to you in January
by Geoffrey Cox is not in the University's best interest. Before finalizing plans that would result in the permanent elimination
of much-valued play areas and natural open spaces in our community — for the sake of 36 units that few young faculty will be
able to afford — we urge you to consider alternatives that would represent a more comprehensive planning approach.
Specifically, we ask you to:
(1) Suspend all work on the proposed "infill" developments, pending investigation of the costs and benefits oF the full range
of alternative options including:
a) other sites for housing construction, their capacity, advantages and disadvantages;
b) ways that housing assistance and loan programs for faculty could be improved;
c) policies to reserve housing on Stanford lands for eligible Stanford personnel;
d) policies to give retired faculty and staff priority in Stanford West senior housing;
(2) Report the results of these investigations to all eligible faculty and staff;
(3) After full disclosure and discussion, survey faculty and staff concerning their views of the alternatives;
(4) Strengthen the role of campus residents in the governance of their community.
Over 500 Stanford residents support this request. Their names are listed on the following pages, along with a campus map
showing the broad geographic distribution of households represented.
We want to stress again that we are not against building necessary housing. We are eager to see Stanford's housing needs
addressed, and many campus homeowners are ready to roll up their sleeves and help figure out the best approaches. Many
have already offered constructive suggestions. After all, this is our campus, our institution, and we have shown and will contin
ue to show that we want it to flourish in every way.
But we are for responsible and comprehensive planning that respects the interests of all the constituencies in our community.
Responsible planning, in our view, requires taking into account residents' views concerning proposed developments in their
neighborhoods — in particular the desire to retain sufficient open space and parkland in our community to ensure that these
neighborhoods not only meet the needs of present Stanford families and students but remain an important asset in recruiting
new faculty. We urge you not to preclude the development of a comprehensive and well-integrated campus housing plan by
proceeding in isolation with the "infill" proposal.
We all have much to gain from working together to find housing solutions that serve the interests of the entire University com
munity.
Respectfully, (signatories listed on this page)
