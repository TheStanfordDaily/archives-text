# Biz School is No. 3
## GSB graduates tops in Internet startups, earnings 
### BEN ALEXANDER-BLOCH CONTRIBUTING WRITER 
The Graduate School of
Business was ranked third
behind Harvard and Whar
ton at the University of Penn
sylvania in a recent Financial
Times survey that listed the
world's top business schools.
"Stanford marches to its
own drummer," said Robert

Joss, Business School dean.
"The difference between
[numbers] one and three is
not statistically significant."
The current attractiveness
of Internet start-ups is con
tinuing to give Stanford an
edge in the market, however.
The Financial limes men
tioned that Stanford's Busi
ness School graduates report
the highest salaries in the
business sector for the class of
1996, as the 1 1 Stanford
grads that responded earned
an average of $303,900 each.


JOSH HANER/The Stanford Daily
London's Financial Times ranked the Graduate School of Business third among the world's business
schools, behind Harvard and Wharton at University of Pennsylvania. U.S. News & World Report, how
ever, places Stanford at number one.
"Siuveys in general have trou
ble evaluating the value of stock
options in start-ups," Joss said.
As a large percentage of Stan
ford MBAs enter start-ups, this
evaluation may ailed the rankings.
In addition, the Financial
limes' survey placed a heavy
weight on the international ele
ments of the MBA programs,

which helped Wharton move from
fourth place to second in the rank
ings. displacing Stanford and Co
lumbia.
I lie Financial limes comment
ed 011 the increasing strength of
many European business schools.
I lie London Business School, for
example, managed to rank in the
top ten for the first time, placing
eighth.
"There are three reasons why
Kuropean business schools have be
come more competitive," Joss said.
"First, there is now more accep

tance of business education in the
Kuropean community and, there
fore, it has more legitimacy. Second,
there are more and more available
faculty, so more academic resources
are accessible. Third, students are
realizing that they do not have to
cross the Atlantic anymore to get a
competitive business education."
According to Joss, the relation
ship between the U.S. business
schools and those in Kurope is one
of friendly competition.
Joss said, however, that Stan
lord only lost one student last year

to a European school, the Insead
in Fontainebleau near Paris.
The competition between Har
vard and Stanford, though, seems
as strong as ever. Joss jokingly de
clined to mention I farvard's name
during the interview.
"Yes, 1 am aware we placed be
hind that other school — what is its
name again?" Joss said.
In the often-cited U.S. News
and World Report magazine rank
ings, the Business School was
ranked first, ahead of Harvard.
