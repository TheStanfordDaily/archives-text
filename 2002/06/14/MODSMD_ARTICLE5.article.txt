September
On Sept. 14, Gerhard Casper
announced his resignation from
the post of University president,
effective Aug. 31,2000. After eight
years in the position, Casper in
tends to return to teaching after a
one-year sabbatical.
After five years of serving as
Stanford University Dean of Edu
cation, Richard J. Shavelson an
nounced his resignation on Sept.
21. After he stepped down on Aug.
31, 2000, he became a fellow at the
Center for the Advanced Study in
Behavioral Science.
October
Laurence Hoagland, the first
president of the Stanford Man
agement Company (which man
ages Stanford's endowment),
announced in early October
that he would step down after
nine years of service. He became
the treasurer of the Hewlett
Foundation.
Silicon Valley businessman
and former Computer Science
Professor Jim Clark announced
on Oct. 26 that he would give
Stanford $150 million, the
largest single donation to the
University since its founding.
The donation will fund the new
interdisciplinary Program in
Biomedical Engineering, also
known as Bio-X.
On Oct. 28, University Presi
dent Gerhard Casper decided to
dissolve the merger between UC-
San Francisco and Stanford
Health Care. The decision ended
the tumultuous two-year history
for UCSF-Stanford.
November
The Stanford football team
clinched a berth in the Rose Bowl
after defeating California in the
BigC iame on Nov. 20.

January
On Jan.l, the Wisconsin Bad
gers defeated the Stanford Cardi
nal 17-9 at the Rose Bowl in
Pasadena, Calif. This was the first
time the football team had made it
to the Rose Bowl in 28 years.
After more than six years of lit
igating on the University's behalf,
General Counsel Michael Roster
decided in early January to step
down to take on a job at a major
banking group. He handed over
his position to Debra Zumwalt.
Dean of Admissions and Finan
cial Aid Robert Kinnally an
nounced on Jan. 17 that he
planned to step down at the end of
the academic year to become a
Catholic priest. Kinnally had been
at Stanford since the fall of 1997.
February
On Feb. 8, after almost 40 years
at Stanford and 15 with the Office
of Undergraduate Admissions, Se
nior Director of Undergraduate
Admission Jon Reider resigned to
become the director of college
counseling at San Francisco Uni
versity High School.
Reider cited his desire to culti
vate closer relationships with stu
dents and the lack of money and
space available in the Office of Ad
missions as his reasons for leaving.
In light of increasing alcohol
violations during Admit Week
end, the Office of Undergradu
ate Admissions announced in
early February that it had devel
oped a preliminary Admit Week
end behavioral contract for all
prospective freshmen. The
agreement asked freshmen to
comply with expected behavior
in six major areas: respect for
others, alcohol, illegal drugs,
smoking, weapons and damage
to property. Violation of the stan
dards could, at worst, result in
withdrawal of Stanford's offer of
admission.

As a result of worsening service
and student complaints, the ASSU
held a post-office forum on Feb. 15
in which students and staff aired
their grievances to post-office offi
cials present at the meeting. It was
announced at the meeting that the
Office of the Inspector General
would conduct a criminal investi
gation and audit of the post office.
March
Students voiced complaints on
the possible houses that Residen
tial Education proposed for the Pi
Beta Phi sorority, which was the
only Greek organization to receive
housing this year, and the third
sorority in the past three years to
receive housing.
The possible houses listed were
Bob, Narnia, Whitman or (>8()
Lomita, which raised student con
cern based on the houses' popular
ity. The Pi Phis eventually were
awarded Whitman.
On March 2, junior Alex Newell
was named Tree for the following
academic year. She is the first
woman since 1985 to be named
Tree.
On March 27, The Daily report
ed that postal workers were caught
throwing away mail by freshman
Michael Osofsky, who took photos
of the workers loading mail into
dumpsters. While postal workers
said they were authorized to throw
away undeliverable bulk mail, they
were also caught throwing away
first-class mail, a felony. Postal offi
cials in San Francisco began an ex
ternal investigation of the post of
fice as a result.
The admit rate for the Class of
2004 dipped to 111 percent. Stan
ford accepted 1,921 students,
roughly 12 percent of the overall
regular decision applicant pool of
16,273. Combined with early deri
sion admits, which numbered 470,
Stanford let in a total of 2,391 stu
dents out of 18,360.

April
Provost John Hennessy was
named the next president of Stan
ford, succeeding outgoing Uni
versity President Gerhard Casper.
I lennessy, a professor of electrical
engineering and computer sci
ence, has been at Stanford since
1977. Hennessy was provost fora
year, having replaced former
Provost Condoleezza Rice in 1999.
Before becoming provost, he
spent three years as dean of the
School of Engineering.
In the wake of the Stanford-
UCSF merger break-up, Stan

ford Hospital and Lucile Packard
Children's Hospital named new
chief executive officers. Malinda
Mitchell took over as the new
CEO of Stanford Hospital last
Monday. The new CEO for the
Children's Hospital is Christo
pher Dawes.
UCLA educator Deborah Jane
Stipek was named the next dean of
the School of Education, becoming
only the second woman to be
named dean of a school in the Uni
versity's history.
May
Junior Seth Newton was elected

ASSU president in a runoff elec
tion, which had to be held after
only eight votes separated Newton
and opposing candidate Ben Liu
in the initial election.
Mariann Byerwalter, vice presi
dent for business affairs and chief
financial officer, announced she
would step down june 30.
June
Philosophy Prof. John
Etchemendy was named Uni
versity provost. He is replacing
current Provost John Hen
nessy, who stepped up to the
presidency.

September
On Sept. 14, Gerhard Casper
announced his resignation from
the post of University president,
effective Aug. 31,2000. After eight
years in the position, Casper in
tends to return to teaching after a
one-year sabbatical.
After five years of serving as
Stanford University Dean of Edu
cation, Richard J. Shavelson an
nounced his resignation on Sept.
21. After he stepped down on Aug.
31, 2000, he became a fellow at the
Center for the Advanced Study in
Behavioral Science.
October
Laurence Hoagland, the first
president of the Stanford Man
agement Company (which man
ages Stanford's endowment),
announced in early October
that he would step down after
nine years of service. He became
the treasurer of the Hewlett
Foundation.
Silicon Valley businessman
and former Computer Science
Professor Jim Clark announced
on Oct. 26 that he would give
Stanford $150 million, the
largest single donation to the
University since its founding.
The donation will fund the new
interdisciplinary Program in
Biomedical Engineering, also
known as Bio-X.
On Oct. 28, University Presi
dent Gerhard Casper decided to
dissolve the merger between UC-
San Francisco and Stanford
Health Care. The decision ended
the tumultuous two-year history
for UCSF-Stanford.

The Stanford football team

after defeating California in the

January
On Jan.l, the Wisconsin Bad
gers defeated the Stanford Cardi
nal 17-9 at the Rose Bowl in
Pasadena, Calif. This was the first
time the football team had made it
to the Rose Bowl in 28 years.
After more than six years of lit
igating on the University's behalf,
General Counsel Michael Roster
decided in early January to step
down to take on a job at a major
banking group. He handed over
his position to Debra Zumwalt.
Dean of Admissions and Finan
cial Aid Robert Kinnally an
nounced on Jan. 17 that he
planned to step down at the end of
the academic year to become a
Catholic priest. Kinnally had been
at Stanford since the fall of 1997.
February
On Feb. 8, after almost 40 years
at Stanford and 15 with the Office
of Undergraduate Admissions, Se
nior Director of Undergraduate
Admission Jon Reider resigned to
become the director of college
counseling at San Francisco Uni
versity High School.
Reider cited his desire to culti
vate closer relationships with stu
dents and the lack of money and
space available in the Office of Ad
missions as his reasons for leaving.
In light of increasing alcohol
violations during Admit Week
end, the Office of Undergradu
ate Admissions announced in
early February that it had devel
oped a preliminary Admit Week
end behavioral contract for all
prospective freshmen. The
agreement asked freshmen to
comply with expected behavior
in six major areas: respect for
others, alcohol, illegal drugs,
smoking, weapons and damage
to property. Violation of the stan
dards could, at worst, result in
withdrawal of Stanford's offer of
admission.

As a result of worsening service
and student complaints, the ASSU
held a post-office forum on Feb. 15
in which students and staff aired
their grievances to post-office offi
cials present at the meeting. It was
announced at the meeting that the
Office of the Inspector General
would conduct a criminal investi
gation and audit of the post office.
